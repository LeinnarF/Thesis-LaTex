\section{Results and Discussion}

The evaluation of \textit{Lakad} includes the algorithmic benchmarking required for efficient route optimization, followed by the assessment of the system’s user acceptability and technical software quality. These findings demonstrate how integrating mathematical optimization models with localized data can effectively address the uneven promotion of tourist destinations in Bulacan.

\subsection{Route Optimization Algorithm Selection}

The comparative benchmarking of the seven metaheuristic algorithms revealed a distinct trade-off between solution quality (accuracy) and computational efficiency (runtime). Table \ref{tab:acc} presents the average relative error of each algorithm compared to the known optimal solutions of the TSPLIB instances. The Genetic Algorithm (GA) consistently produced the highest solution quality, achieving an exceptional average error of 0.10\%. Simulated Annealing (SA) and Ant Colony Optimization (ACO) also performed competitively, yielding average errors of 1.59\% and 2.50\%, respectively.

However, as demonstrated in Table \ref{tab:runtime}, GA's superior accuracy comes at a severe computational cost. As the problem size increased, GA's runtime scaled exponentially, requiring over 196 seconds to complete the \textit{berlin52} instance. Conversely, the Elephant Herding Optimizer (EHO) maintained the fastest execution (under 0.5 seconds across all tests) but suffered from rapidly degrading accuracy on larger datasets, making it unreliable for complex itinerary optimization. SA and the Gray Wolf Optimizer (GWO) maintained consistently short runtimes across all instances.

To objectively resolve this trade-off, the Simple Additive Weighting (SAW) method was applied, assigning equal weights to both the normalized solution quality and runtime efficiency. As shown in Table \ref{tab:rank}, Simulated Annealing (SA) achieved the premier rank across the majority of instances. GA's prolonged execution times penalized its SAW score heavily, dropping it to 6th place overall, while EHO secured 2nd place due to its speed but lacked the necessary precision for practical routing.

Consequently, Simulated Annealing (SA) was selected as the designated routing engine for the \textit{Lakad} application. This result aligns with the assertions of \textcite{Wadi2025}, which dictate that heuristic models balancing rapid execution with near-optimal accuracy are vastly superior for resource-constrained mobile environments compared to exact methods or highly complex genetic algorithms that risk critical resource exhaustion. SA ensures that users receive highly optimized travel sequences within a responsive, real-time time frame.

\textit{Lakad} was developed as a tourists spot explorer and itinerary planner with route optimization capabilities. It features a dual-interface architecture tailored for both tourists and system administrators. The app successfully bridge the gap between localized tourism data and advanced optimization models.

Figure \ref{fig:2} details the parameter initialization sequence for AGAM itinerary generation. This solves the problem of manual itinerary planning which can be time-consuming and overwhelming for users, especially those who are not familiar with the area. Users define their target municipalities, prefered type of tourist spots, maximum travel distance, and the upper bound for stopovers. These inputs serve as the primary constraints for the AGAM fitness function (discussed in Section 2.2). Upon execution, the system processes these multi-objective parameters to dynamically generate a highly customized POI sequence that maximizes user interest while strictly adhering to the defined travel budget.

Once AGAM generates the optimal set of destinations, the system address the routing challenge (the Traveling Salesman Problem) via the optimization feature shown in Figure \ref{fig:3}. Utilizing the Simulated Annealing (SA) algorithm, the system evaluates the distance matrix corresponding to the selected POIs. The SA systematically reorders the sequence of destinations to minimize the global travel distance. The interface shows the before and after optimization of the generated itinerary, providing feedback to the user the total distance and estimated time saved through the algorithmic intervention.

To ensure the long-term maintainability and scalability of \textit{Lakad}, a dedicated administrative interface was developed (Figure \ref{fig:4}). The admin mode provides a dashboard where administrators can see the summary and reports of the application, including the total number of itineraries, tourist spots, and kilometers planned in the itineraries. These analytics offers data-driven insights that can assist LGU policymakers in evaluating tourism distribution.

Furthermore, administrators can also manage tourist spots by adding or updating information about them, as well as managing the distance matrix used for route optimization. This ensures maintainability and scalability of \textit{Lakad}, allowing it to adapt to changing tourism trends and user needs while providing a seamless experience for both tourists and administrators.

The TAM survey results presented in Table \ref{tam1} displays the mean scores and standard deviations for each construct. The constructs achieved high mean scores, interpreted as highly acceptable, implying that users found the Lakad to be useful and easy to use while they had a positive attitude towards the application. The application received exceptional marks in three categories where perceived usefulness achieved 4.7053, perceived ease of use achieved 4.7067, and attitude towards using achieved 4.7200. The results show that the application can effectively meet the users’ needs and expectations. However, the behavioral intention to use scored slightly lower than the other constructs but still achieved a high mean score of 4.5556 which indicates that users will likely continue to use the application in future or recommend it to other people.

All constructs resulted in low standard deviations, measuring between 0.3435 and 0.4740. The narrow distribution of data points shows a strong consensus among respondents because most users reported similar positive perceptions rather than opposing or different experiences. The lowest variance is perceived usefulness with 0.3435, showing that users almost completely agreed on the application’s usefulness. The total TAM score of 4.6719 implies that users accepted Lakad positively.

The acceptability scores also supports the claims of \textcite{Yochum2020} that AGAM's framework of multi-objective optimization problem provides users with a richer and unique experience, a goal that is aimed to be achieved by DOT's tourism development plan.

Lakad achieved its highest mean score through its Functional Suitability which received a score of 4.8636. The application’s current exclusive use on Android devices results in its lowest score for Compatibility which received a mean score of 4.6591.

The standard deviations offer further insights into the experts’ evaluations, which demonstrate a narrow range from 0.2447 to 0.4973. Functional Sustainability achieved the lowest standard deviation of 0.2447 while showing the highest mean results because all IT professionals nearly agreed about the application’s ability to function according to its core features. These evaluation scores in acceptability and software quality imply that Lakad proves to be a capable tool that offers not only personalization, routing, and management but also provide it as one free, localized solution, a user-centered approach that previous studies struggled to deliver.
